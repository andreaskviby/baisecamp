## Hi, I'm Andreas Kviby

I've been writing code since I was a kid. My first real programs were on a Commodore 64, typing in BASIC listings from magazines, then modifying them to see what would break. That curiosity — *what happens if I change this?* — never left. It's still how I approach software today.

Over the decades, the languages changed. The frameworks changed. The platforms changed. But the feeling of making a machine do something useful, something that wasn't possible before you wrote those lines — that feeling stayed exactly the same.

## The early years

I grew up in Sweden, and like a lot of kids in the 80s, I was fascinated by computers before I really understood what they were. The Commodore 64 was magic. You could type words and the screen would respond. You could make things move. You could build worlds.

By the time I was a teenager, I'd moved from BASIC to Pascal, then to C. I taught myself from books, from magazines, from the trial-and-error of breaking things and putting them back together. There was no Stack Overflow. There was no YouTube. If you wanted to learn, you read the manual, or you figured it out yourself.

That era shaped how I think about software. I learned that documentation matters. I learned that understanding the *why* is more important than copying the *what*. I learned that the best code is the code that doesn't need to exist — the problem you solve by thinking harder, not by writing more.

## The web era

When the web arrived, everything changed. Suddenly you could build something and put it in front of people anywhere in the world, instantly. I dove in headfirst.

I learned HTML by viewing source. I learned CSS by breaking layouts and rebuilding them. I learned JavaScript when it was still considered a toy language, before anyone imagined it running servers or building mobile apps. I learned PHP when it was the wild west of web development — messy, powerful, and incredibly productive.

Eventually I found my way to Laravel, and it felt like coming home. A framework that respected developers. A framework with opinions, but not dogma. A framework that let you move fast without sacrificing craft. I've built dozens of applications with Laravel over the years, and I still reach for it first when starting something new.

## Basecamp, since 2007

In 2007, I discovered Basecamp.

It wasn't my first project management tool. I'd tried others — heavyweight enterprise software, complicated systems with more features than anyone could use, tools that felt like they were designed by committees who had never actually shipped a project.

Basecamp was different. It was simple. It was opinionated. It did less, but it did it *well*. You could start using it in five minutes and actually get work done.

I've been using Basecamp ever since. Through Basecamp Classic, through Basecamp 2, through Basecamp 3, and now through Basecamp 4 (or "the new Basecamp" as they call it). Nearly two decades of todos, message boards, schedules, and hill charts. Nearly two decades of projects shipped, teams coordinated, and clients kept in the loop.

What I love about Basecamp isn't just the software — it's the philosophy. The idea that software should calm things down, not stress them out. The idea that you don't need real-time everything, that async communication is often better, that "good enough today" beats "perfect someday." The Basecamp team — Jason Fried, DHH, and everyone at 37signals — has shaped how I think about building products more than almost anyone else in the industry.

I've read every book they've written. REWORK. REMOTE. It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work. Shape Up. Getting Real. These aren't just business books to me — they're manuals for a saner way to work.

## The mobile era

When smartphones arrived, I learned iOS development. Then Android. Then I watched the mobile landscape fragment and consolidate and fragment again.

Eventually I found Flutter, and it felt like the web era all over again — a single codebase that runs everywhere, a framework that's opinionated but flexible, a community that moves fast. I've been building with Flutter for years now, and it's become one of my primary tools.

The common thread across all these eras: I've always gravitated toward frameworks and tools that let one person (or a small team) punch above their weight. I don't want to manage a team of twenty. I want to build things with the power of twenty, using leverage and good tools.

## The AI era

And now we're in a new era. The AI era.

I started experimenting with large language models as soon as they became accessible. At first, I used them like everyone else — as a fancy autocomplete, a research assistant, a rubber duck that talks back.

But then I started thinking differently. What if the AI wasn't just a tool I used manually? What if it was a *teammate* — something that could pick up tasks, do work, and report back?

That's what this blog is about. I've built a system where AI agents integrate directly with Basecamp. My clients file todos the same way they always did. They assign them to a teammate called Auto Andy. And Auto Andy does the work — reads the requirements, writes the code, posts the result, marks the task complete.

It sounds like science fiction, but it's running right now, on a Mac mini in my office. The five-part series on this blog explains exactly how it works.

## Why "Baisecamp"?

The name is a portmanteau: **B**asecamp + **AI** = Baisecamp.

It's a little silly, but it captures what I'm exploring here: the intersection of a tool I've loved for nearly two decades and the most significant shift in software development since the web.

Basecamp has always been about making work calmer and more organized. AI agents are about making work more automated and more powerful. I believe these two ideas complement each other beautifully. The structure and sanity of Basecamp, combined with the capability of AI agents, creates something greater than either alone.

## Looking forward to Basecamp 5

As I write this, I'm eagerly waiting for Basecamp 5.

Every major version of Basecamp has been a meaningful evolution. Not change for the sake of change — thoughtful, opinionated improvements based on years of experience and customer feedback. The 37signals team doesn't ship often, but when they do, it's worth the wait.

I have no idea what Basecamp 5 will look like. But I trust the team. They've earned that trust over eighteen years of building software that respects my time, my attention, and my sanity.

Whatever Basecamp 5 brings, I'll be there on day one. And I'll be figuring out how to make my AI agents work even better with it.

## What I believe about software

After all these years, here's what I believe:

**Simple is hard.** Anyone can build something complicated. Building something simple that actually works is much harder. The best software feels obvious in retrospect.

**Tools should amplify, not overwhelm.** A good tool makes you more capable. A bad tool makes you spend your time managing the tool. Every feature is a trade-off.

**Small teams beat big teams.** Two people who care deeply will outship twenty people who don't. Hire slow, stay small, use leverage.

**Async beats sync.** Real-time communication is overrated. Most things don't need an immediate response. Give people space to think, and they'll do better work.

**Documentation is love.** Writing things down is how you scale yourself. If it's not written down, it doesn't exist.

**Automate the boring parts.** Repetitive work is work you shouldn't be doing. Every hour spent automating a task is an hour that pays dividends forever.

**Ship it.** Perfection is the enemy of done. Get something in front of users. Learn from their feedback. Iterate.
